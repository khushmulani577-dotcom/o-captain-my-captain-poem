About the Poem

Written in 1865 as an elegy for President Abraham Lincoln, "O Captain! My Captain!" remains one of Walt Whitman's most cherished works. The poem uses the metaphor of a ship's captain to represent Lincoln, who guided the nation through the turbulent waters of the Civil War, only to perish just as victory was achieved.

Whitman, known for his free verse, surprised readers with this poem's conventional rhyme and meter‚Äîa departure that made it instantly popular. The floral motifs in this presentation reflect Whitman's own love of nature, which permeates much of his work.

The Complete Poem

O Captain! my Captain! our fearful trip is done,

The ship has weather'd every rack, the prize we sought is won,

The port is near, the bells I hear, the people all exulting,

While follow eyes the steady keel, the vessel grim and daring;

But O heart! heart! heart!

O the bleeding drops of red,

Where on the deck my Captain lies,

Fallen cold and dead.

O Captain! my Captain! rise up and hear the bells;

Rise up‚Äîfor you the flag is flung‚Äîfor you the bugle trills,

For you bouquets and ribbon'd wreaths‚Äîfor you the shores a-crowding,

For you they call, the swaying mass, their eager faces turning;

Here Captain! dear father!

This arm beneath your head!

It is some dream that on the deck,

You've fallen cold and dead.

My Captain does not answer, his lips are pale and still,

My father does not feel my arm, he has no pulse nor will,

The ship is anchor'd safe and sound, its voyage closed and done,

From fearful trip, the victor ship, comes in with object won;

Exult, O shores, and ring, O bells!

But I, with mournful tread,

Walk the deck my Captain lies,

Fallen cold and dead.

Legacy & Interpretation

The poem's enduring popularity comes from its emotional directness and universal themes of leadership, loss, and bittersweet victory. The floral motifs in this presentation honor Whitman's own poetic style‚Äîhis work often celebrated nature's cyclical patterns as metaphors for human experience.

Educators note the poem remains valuable for teaching poetic devices: its extended metaphor (ship as nation), apostrophe (direct address to the captain), and juxtaposition of celebratory images with mournful reality. The floral borders and soft colors reflect the poem's Victorian origins while modernizing its presentation.
